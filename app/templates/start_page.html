The Planner's Pledge is an effort to reform the planning profession by introducing a well-defined quality standard that has, until now, been conspicuously missing. Planners signing the pledge make a commitment to pursue a collection of practices that have been demonstrated to produce superior outcomes for society and the planet. By establishing such a standard and bending practice in its direction, professional planners can once again merit a leadership role in the creation and physical evolution of American communities.

THE PLEDGE

As practitioners of a discipline that has advocated for what it believed to be best practices while inevitably advancing the dominant ideology of its society, we professional planners wish to formally apologize for the significant role that our profession has played in creating the unsustainable suburban sprawl development pattern that now constitutes most of the American built environment, reinforcing race and class segregation while burdening the vast majority of citizens with the mandate of car ownership.

To put it bluntly, planners have contributed to the creation of a landscape that separates us ruthlessly both from different activities and from different people, such that we are inordinately dependent on our automobiles and less connected to one another. While citizens of other countries enjoy daily life in walkable, mixed-use, diverse neighborhoods, the typical American lives in a cluster of economically identical single-family houses where it is impossible to walk to work, school, shopping, recreation, or worship.

The outcomes of this pattern of growth could not be more profound. We have worsened public health, lost economic resiliency, exacerbated inequity, and undermined the social fabric of our communities as our daily driving cooks the planet.

This changes today. Professional planners, who sit in city and town halls across the nation and create the rules that shape our growth, being directly responsible for the current circumstances, must take responsibility for changing them. Apologies are not enough. We must step forward with a new model of professional conduct designed to undo nearly a century of misdirection. That model is summarized in the pledge below.

It will take many years to reverse the zoning laws and transportation standards that created and reinforce our segregated, car-dependent landscape. Politics will continue to favor business as usual. The homebuilding and roadbuilding industries will keep pushing for more of the same and will keep funding politicians who do their bidding. But planners, as a profession, can take a stand. In some places we will be effective in changing the rules—it is already happening in Minneapolis, Seattle, and elsewhere—and every place makes a difference.

The collection of mandates that follows is far from comprehensive, but addresses several dozen aspects of planning practice that are most often gotten wrong, causing the greatest harm to our society and the planet. When a majority of planners have the knowledge and the courage to get these things right, the profession will achieve the positive outcomes it needs to regain the public trust and enter a new era of civic leadership.

Sign the Pledge
Whereas:

  1. The vast majority of the American built environment is car-dependent, where basic needs like schools, parks, and grocery stores are unreachable on foot.
  2. Car-dependent planning is destructive to public health, the economy, the environment, and the social fabric.
  3. Most negative impacts of car dependence will not be alleviated by electrification, especially the wasteful consumption patterns caused by decentralization.
  4. Planning around the car is a self-fulfilling prophesy: traffic studies and parking minimums perpetuate and worsen the very car dependence that they hope to mitigate.
  5. Use-based (Euclidean) zoning creates car dependence.
  6. The current built environment was created on a foundation of racial inequity and environmental injustice, with the clearly stated intention of segregating communities of color.
  7. Single-family zoning, created to perpetuate racial segregation in the face of civil rights laws, is morally bankrupt and detrimental to correcting past wrongs.
  8. Income diversity within neighborhoods is a social good that the market will not provide unless required to do so by law.
  9. Affordable housing is not truly affordable if it requires its residents to own cars.
  10. A dire nationwide housing shortage requires decisive changes to land-use and tax policies.
  11. Primary schooling is best provided where it can be reached on foot. The same is true of daycare, playgrounds, parks, and other municipal amenities.
  12. Walkable, bikeable communities provide residents with a superior quality of life and must be acknowledged as an unmitigated good.
  13. Achieving walkability depends on the creation of a physical framework in which walking is truly useful, safe, comfortable, and interesting.
  14. Walkable, mixed-use development is not possible within a hierarchical system of collector and local roads, and requires a porous network of small blocks.
  15. The proliferation of high-speed street networks characterized by wide lanes, excess lanes, and the overuse of traffic signals has led to an epidemic of pedestrian and cyclist deaths.
  16. Coordination of frequent transit service with concentrations of housing and jobs is the foundation of good planning.
  17. Street trees are an essential component of the public realm, key to enabling walkability and mitigating climate impacts.
  18. Historic buildings connect us to our past and shape an elevated public realm that invites walking.
  19. The city planning profession, currently 2/3 male and 3/4 white, does not fully reflect the demographics of its public clientele.
  20. As Jane Jacobs stressed, “cities have the ability of providing something for everybody, only because, and only when, they are created by everybody.”

I pledge that I will encourage:

  1. A fully inclusive community planning process that strives for representative participation and works to include those who have historically been underrepresented in such efforts.
  2. More housing, at varied price points, in walkable areas;
  3. Dense development adjacent to frequent transit routes and vice versa;
  4. The elimination of single-family-only zoning in all locations;
  5. Accessory Dwelling Units (ADUs) buildable by right in all locations;
  6. Inclusionary zoning and similar programs that improve neighborhood diversity;
  7. Anti-displacement strategies such as community land trusts, mortgage down-payment assistance, and property tax abatement in all areas planned for improvement;
  8. Property tax policies that accurately reflect the higher infrastructure cost of low-density areas;
  9. Property tax policies that discourage speculation by taxing land value rather than buildings;
  10. Coordinated development of blocks to address parking, stormwater, and other collective issues collectively rather than on a lot-by-lot basis;
  11. The elimination of on-site parking requirements;
  12. Decoupling parking fees from apartment rents;
  13. Pricing on-street parking in line with its value;
  14. The design of any new parking lots or structures as convertible to a future residential or commercial use;
  15. Transportation Demand Management tools like employee transit passes, bike subsidies, and cash payouts in lieu of parking;
  16. Docked bikeshare programs;
  17. Sizing and locating schools such that most students can reach them on foot or bike;
  18. Locating daycare, playgrounds, and other amenities to be easily reached on foot or bike;
  19. Designing all new street systems as porous networks of small blocks rather than hierarchical systems of collector and local roads.
  20. Adopting Vision Zero and other policies that focus on safe street design rather than driver error;
  21. Engineering new roads and restriping existing roads to a design speed no higher than the desired vehicle speed;
  22. Requiring all new thoroughfares to include low-stress micromobility routes, either in protected lanes or mixing with truly low-speed traffic;
  23. Sizing firefighting equipment to fit community streets and not vice versa;
  24. Reverting multilane one-way street networks back to two-way travel;
  25. Replacing traffic signals with lower-speed intersection controls such as all-way stops;
  26. Congestion-pricing crowded roadways;
  27. Replacing decrepit elevated or depressed highways with multimodal surface boulevards;
  28. Pedestrianizing main streets after successful trial periods;
  29. Creating a continuous tree canopy above all thoroughfares and parking lots; and
  30. The adaptive reuse of older buildings.

I pledge that I will discourage:

  1. Policies that further the growth of car-dependent places except in ways that reduce their car dependency;
  2. Policies that serve, by intention or accident, to reinforce racial or economic segregation;
  3. Policies that encourage the construction of low-density or exclusive housing in transit-served locations;
  4. The creation of any neighborhood plan without first engaging the local community—prior to design;
  5. The consolidation of schools or parks into larger facilities;
  6. The creation of any new throughfare that does not safely welcome pedestrians and cyclists;
  7. The building of any new highways or highway lanes;
  8. The use of traffic studies to either limit new development or mandate new driving lanes;
  9. The use of traffic LOS (Level of Service) as a principal determinant of community form;
  10. The use of environmental impact studies (or similar) to stall or derail pedestrian, cycling, or transit facilities;
  11. The deployment of any driving technology (such as partial autonomy) that limits the safety and freedom of pedestrians and cyclists;
  12. Property tax policies that discourage the redevelopment of vacant properties or surface parking lots;
  13. Any parking lot located between a building facade and the enfronting sidewalk;
  14. Any parking structure facing a sidewalk without another use at ground level; and
  15. The destruction of any significant sidewalk-facing building facade more than 100 years old.

In signing this pledge, I commit myself to pursuing a fundamentally changed planning profession, one that plays no role in advancing car dependence or social segregation.
